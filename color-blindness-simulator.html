About Color Vision Deficiencies

Protanopia

Affects the red cones in the eye. People with protanopia have difficulty distinguishing between red and green colors. Reds appear darker and may look black or brown.

Deuteranopia

Affects the green cones in the eye. Similar to protanopia but greens appear lighter. This is the most common type of color blindness, affecting about 6% of males.

Tritanopia

Affects the blue cones in the eye. People with tritanopia have difficulty distinguishing between blue and yellow colors. Blues appear greener and yellows may appear violet.

Achromatopsia

Complete color blindness, where people see only in shades of gray. This is a rare condition affecting all three types of cones in the eye.

Design Tips for Accessibility

  • Use both color and patterns/symbols to convey information in charts and graphs
  • Ensure text has sufficient contrast against background colors (4.5:1 ratio minimum)
  • Avoid color combinations that are problematic for colorblind users (red-green, blue-purple)
  • Test your designs with tools like this simulator to ensure accessibility
  • Provide text labels or alternative indicators for color-coded information
